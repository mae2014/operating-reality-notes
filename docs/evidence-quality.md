# Evidence Quality

Evidence quality refers to the degree to which available information is reliable, traceable, timely, complete, and decision-useful.

Evidence quality is not the same as data availability.

A system may have large volumes of data and still lack evidence strong enough to support a responsible decision.

## Why evidence quality matters

Decisions often fail not because information is absent, but because the available information is weak, delayed, incomplete, poorly verified, or misunderstood.

When evidence quality is poor, decision-makers may act with more confidence than the situation deserves.

A responsible decision system should make the strength and limits of evidence visible.

## Core dimensions

### 1. Traceability

Can the evidence be traced back to its source?

Traceability helps determine where information came from, how it was collected, who handled it, and whether it can be reviewed.

### 2. Timeliness

Is the evidence recent enough to support the decision?

Evidence can be accurate and still lose decision value if it arrives too late.

### 3. Completeness

Does the evidence cover the relevant scope?

Incomplete evidence may create blind spots, especially when missing information is treated as if it does not matter.

### 4. Verification

Can the evidence be checked, confirmed, or cross-referenced?

Verification reduces the risk of acting on weak, manipulated, misunderstood, or ungrounded information.

### 5. Consistency

Do the signals align, or do they contradict each other?

Contradictory evidence should not be ignored. It should be surfaced and explained.

### 6. Relevance

Is the evidence relevant to the specific decision being made?

Information may be true without being useful for the decision at hand.

### 7. Decision usefulness

Does the evidence support action, caution, escalation, or delay?

Decision-useful evidence does not need to remove all uncertainty. It must make the uncertainty visible enough for responsible judgment.

## Evidence quality levels

### Strong evidence

Evidence is traceable, timely, sufficiently complete, verified, relevant, and aligned with the decision being considered.

### Limited evidence

Evidence supports partial interpretation, but contains gaps, delays, uncertainty, or verification limits.

Limited evidence may support cautious action, narrower scope, additional controls, or further review.

### Weak evidence

Evidence is missing, delayed, contradictory, unverifiable, or poorly connected to the decision.

Weak evidence should trigger caution, escalation, delay, or redesign of the decision.

## Principle

A decision-grade system should not only ask whether information exists.

It should ask whether the available evidence is strong enough for the decision being made.
