# Decision Boundaries

A decision boundary defines where available evidence is strong enough to support action, where caution is required, and where a decision should be delayed, revised, or escalated.

## Why decision boundaries matter

Organizations often treat evidence as if it were either present or absent.

In practice, evidence quality exists on a spectrum.

A dataset, report, dashboard, field update, or operational signal may exist without being strong enough to support a responsible decision.

The purpose of a decision boundary is to make this distinction visible.

## Three decision zones

### 1. Action zone

The evidence is strong enough to support a defined decision.

This does not mean certainty is perfect.

It means the evidence is sufficiently traceable, timely, complete, and verified for the level of risk involved.

### 2. Caution zone

The evidence supports limited interpretation, but not full confidence.

This may happen when information is delayed, incomplete, partially verified, or operationally unstable.

In this zone, the decision may require narrowing the scope, adding safeguards, requesting more proof, or reducing confidence.

### 3. Stop or escalate zone

The evidence is too weak, contradictory, unverifiable, or unstable to support responsible action.

In this zone, the decision should be delayed, escalated, redesigned, or rejected until the evidence layer improves.

## Key questions

A decision boundary should help answer:

1. What is known?
2. What is missing?
3. What is unverifiable?
4. What is contradictory?
5. What assumptions are being made?
6. What level of confidence is justified?
7. What decision is the evidence strong enough to support?
8. What decision would be irresponsible under current evidence conditions?

## Principle

A responsible decision system should not only support action.

It should also know when not to act.

When evidence quality weakens, the system should degrade into caution, not false certainty.
