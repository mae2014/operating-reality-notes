# Definitions

This document defines the core terms used across these notes.

## Operating reality

Operating reality refers to what actually happens when a strategy, process, project, or decision meets real-world execution conditions.

It includes delays, constraints, informal practices, missing data, contradictory signals, resource gaps, institutional friction, and field-level behavior.

## Evidence quality

Evidence quality refers to the degree to which available information is reliable, traceable, timely, complete, and decision-useful.

Evidence may exist without being strong enough to support a responsible decision.

## Decision-grade evidence

Decision-grade evidence is evidence that is sufficiently structured, bounded, and reliable to support a specific decision under defined conditions.

It does not require perfect certainty. It requires visible assumptions, known limits, and a clear decision boundary.

## Decision boundary

A decision boundary defines where available evidence is strong enough to support action, where caution is required, and where the decision should be delayed, revised, or escalated.

## High-friction environments

High-friction environments are operating contexts where execution is affected by fragmented data, infrastructure gaps, institutional complexity, informal systems, regulatory uncertainty, weak traceability, or limited verification capacity.

## Confidence degradation

Confidence degradation is the process by which a system reduces its confidence level when evidence becomes incomplete, delayed, contradictory, unverifiable, or operationally unstable.
