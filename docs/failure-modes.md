# Failure Modes

This document tracks common failure modes in operating reality, evidence quality, and decision-grade systems.

A failure mode is a recurring way in which a system, process, report, dashboard, or decision layer can become unreliable.

## 1. Data presence mistaken for evidence quality

A dataset, dashboard, or report may exist, but still be incomplete, delayed, poorly verified, or unsuitable for decision-making.

The presence of data should not be treated as proof that the evidence layer is reliable.

## 2. False confidence

False confidence occurs when a system produces a strong conclusion without showing the weakness, incompleteness, or uncertainty of the evidence behind it.

This is especially dangerous when decisions involve capital, risk, compliance, public trust, or operational execution.

## 3. Missing proof hidden by clean presentation

A clean dashboard or polished report can hide missing evidence.

Visual clarity should not be confused with evidentiary strength.

## 4. Contradictory signals ignored

Operational reality often produces signals that do not align.

If contradictory signals are ignored, the system may produce a simplified conclusion that does not reflect the real operating context.

## 5. Timeliness failure

Evidence can become less useful when it arrives too late.

A delayed signal may still be true, but no longer decision-useful.

## 6. Verification gap

A verification gap appears when information is used for decision-making without a clear way to confirm its source, reliability, or chain of custody.

This weakens accountability and increases the risk of poor decisions.

## 7. Field reality loss

Field reality loss occurs when decision systems become too detached from what is actually happening in execution.

This may happen when reporting layers become too abstract, too centralized, or too dependent on formal inputs that do not capture informal operating behavior.

## 8. Assumption invisibility

Decisions often rely on assumptions.

When those assumptions are not visible, decision-makers may mistake interpretation for fact.

A responsible evidence system should make key assumptions explicit.

## 9. Over-automation

Automation can increase speed, but it can also amplify weak evidence.

A system that automates decisions without checking evidence quality may scale false confidence.

## 10. No degradation path

When evidence weakens, the system should reduce confidence, narrow the decision scope, request more proof, or escalate.

A system without a degradation path may continue producing confident outputs even when the evidence layer is unstable.

## Principle

A decision-grade system should not only optimize for speed, clarity, or automation.

It should also identify when its own evidence base is too weak to support responsible action.
